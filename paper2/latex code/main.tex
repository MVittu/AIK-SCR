\title{\textbf{Breathing Techniques and Breath-Regulation Strategies in Sport and Performance Contexts: A Scoping Review}}
\author{Review authors}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
\textbf{Background:} Breathing techniques are used in sport as warm-ups, recovery routines, training stimuli, attentional tools, and psychophysiological regulation strategies. Their shared label is misleading because voluntary hyperventilation, hypoventilation at low lung volume, breath holding, slow-paced breathing, diaphragmatic breathing, pranayama, and composite routines manipulate different physiological targets. \textbf{Objective:} This scoping review maps non-device breathing and breath-regulation strategies in sport and performance contexts, with attention to technique specificity, outcomes, feasibility, and safety. \textbf{Methods:} The review followed PRISMA-ScR principles. Sixty-five reports were charted descriptively. Exploratory syntheses used Hedges' $g$ for extractable efficacy outcomes and risk ratios for extractable dropout and adverse-event data. \textbf{Results:} The corpus contained 65 reports published between 2000 and 2026, with 2,188 participants across extractable samples. Crossover and other acute experimental designs were common, while durable training trials were concentrated in selected intervention families. Hypoventilation and breath-holding protocols were most clearly tied to repeated-sprint, swimming, cycling, and apnea contexts; slow-paced and diaphragmatic protocols were more often used for recovery, autonomic regulation, or precision-performance tasks; voluntary hyperventilation was tested mainly as an acute ergogenic or metabolic strategy. Exploratory pooling was uncertain for both performance ($g=-0.06$, 95\% CI -0.34 to 0.22) and physiological outcomes ($g=0.28$, 95\% CI -0.07 to 0.63). Dropout and adverse-event syntheses did not show clear comparative differences, but reporting was sparse. \textbf{Conclusions:} The evidence does not support a single conclusion about ``breathwork'' in sport. It supports a technique-specific research agenda built around clear protocols, mechanism-matched outcomes, credible comparators, adherence reporting, and prespecified safety monitoring.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Key takeaways}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item The review includes 65 reports, but the evidence is better understood by technique family than by a generic breathwork label.
\item Hypoventilation and breath-holding studies provide the clearest sport-training cluster, especially for repeated-sprint and swimming contexts.
\item Slow-paced, box, coherent, and diaphragmatic routines are more plausibly positioned as recovery, autonomic, attentional, or pre-performance tools than as universal ergogenic aids.
\item Exploratory pooled effects are uncertain and should guide study design rather than practice claims.
\item Feasibility and adverse-event reporting remain too incomplete for strong safety reassurance.

\section{Introduction}
Breathing is both automatic and voluntarily adjustable. Athletes can alter respiratory frequency, depth, inhalation-exhalation ratio, route of breathing, breath-hold timing, and lung volume without external equipment. That makes breath regulation attractive in sport: it can be used before competition, during exertion, between repeated efforts, during recovery, or as a repeated training exposure. It is also a source of conceptual confusion. A pre-sprint hyperventilation protocol, a low-lung-volume hypoventilation session, a box-breathing recovery routine, a pranayama program, and a diaphragmatic warm-up do not share the same mechanism simply because all involve deliberate breathing.

The sport literature already points toward this distinction. Laborde and colleagues separated slow-paced breathing, fast-paced breathing, voluntary hyperventilation, breath holding, and nostril-breathing approaches in their systematic review and meta-analysis, and concluded that intervention duration, technique type, heterogeneity, and risk of bias limited broad claims about physical sport performance \parencite{Laborde31122024}. The present review takes a complementary scoping approach. Instead of asking whether breathing techniques have a single average effect, it asks what has been studied, where the evidence clusters, which outcomes have been used, and whether feasibility and safety are reported well enough to support translation.

This framing is important because the plausible target changes by technique. Voluntary hyperventilation can alter carbon dioxide and acid-base balance before or between high-intensity efforts. Hypoventilation and end-expiratory breath holding are often used to create hypoxic or hypercapnic stress during repeated-sprint work. Slow-paced, coherent, box, and diaphragmatic breathing are more often justified through autonomic regulation, attentional control, and recovery. Glossopharyngeal, hook-breathing, and apnea-related routines belong to still different sport-specific practices. A scoping review is therefore appropriate because it can map these families without forcing them into a single efficacy estimate.

The review questions were: (1) which breathing-technique families have been studied in sport and performance contexts; (2) which populations, designs, outcomes, and intervention doses characterize the evidence base; and (3) what can be learned from extractable efficacy, feasibility, and safety data while preserving technique specificity.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Design and reporting}
This scoping review was designed to map the breadth, structure, and reporting completeness of evidence on breathing techniques in sport and performance contexts. The manuscript is reported according to PRISMA-ScR principles. The review maps the available evidence rather than claiming a single definitive intervention effect. The unit of descriptive synthesis was the individual report. Quantitative syntheses were exploratory adjuncts to the evidence map and used only outcomes for which the required group-level data could be extracted.

\subsection{Protocol and registration}
The review protocol was registered on the Open Science Framework: \url{https://osf.io/3t2wc/overview?view_only=7518720a5f334682b8bb5ebba89b1cbd}.

\subsection{Eligibility criteria}
Eligibility was defined using a Population--Concept--Context structure. Eligible populations included athletes, sport participants, physically active participants, and healthy participants completing performance-relevant exercise or psychophysiological tasks. The concept was a breathing technique, breath-regulation routine, or breathing-related exposure that could be performed without a device that directly altered respiratory load or inspired gas composition. Eligible concepts included hyperventilation, hypoventilation, paced or slow breathing, breath holding/apnea, pranayama or yogic breathing, diaphragmatic breathing, hook breathing, Valsalva-type strategies, and related approaches. Eligible contexts included sport training, laboratory exercise tasks, sport-specific testing, recovery, and performance-relevant psychophysiological outcomes. Source types included primary empirical reports with extractable study-level information; reviews were used for citation searching but were not included as primary evidence rows. The operational inclusion and exclusion criteria are reproduced in the appendix.

\subsection{Information sources and search strategy}
Searches were run in PubMed, Scopus, and SPORTDiscus. The database-specific search strings are reported in the appendix. The searches combined terms for healthy or athletic populations, breathing techniques, sport or performance outcomes, and exclusions for reviews, respiratory muscle training, pulmonary disease, and major clinical respiratory contexts. Additional reports were identified through citation searching, backtracking from relevant reviews, and manual reconciliation during full-text assessment.

\subsection{Selection process}
Records were screened at title/abstract level, followed by full-text assessment of potentially eligible reports. Full texts were assessed against the population, concept, context, and source-type criteria described above. Disagreements and uncertain cases were resolved during eligibility reconciliation before final extraction.

\subsection{Data charting and quality control}
Data were charted for bibliometry, study design, sport context, athlete level, sample size, age, sex distribution, baseline characteristics, breathing category, intervention dose, comparator, efficacy outcomes, uncertainty, feasibility, dropout, adverse events, and safety. Missing or unreported fields were retained as not reported so that denominators and reporting gaps remained explicit. Data-quality checks flagged incomplete efficacy fields, non-positive dispersion values, unclear breathing categories, and sparse feasibility or safety reporting for manual review.

\subsection{Outcome grouping and synthesis}
Descriptive synthesis summarized publication year, study design, participant characteristics, sport context, breathing category, intervention duration, intervention frequency, feasibility, and safety. Outcomes were grouped into performance, physiological, feasibility, and safety domains. Exploratory meta-analysis used Hedges' $g$ for efficacy outcomes when group-level data were extractable. Effect direction was standardized so that positive values favored the breathing intervention; lower-is-better outcomes such as time, reaction time, heart rate, RPE, dyspnea, fatigue, lactate, and decrement scores were reversed. Safety and dropout were synthesized as risk ratios where event data were available. Very imprecise safety estimates were retained in extraction records but excluded from displayed forest plots when the interval made the figure unreadable.

\subsection{Feasibility and safety definitions}
Feasibility fields included adherence, dropout, acceptability when available, and reasons for discontinuation. Safety fields included adverse-event reporting status, adverse-event descriptions, and event counts when extractable. Absence of reported adverse events was not interpreted as evidence that no events occurred unless the source explicitly stated this.

\subsection{Critical appraisal}
Formal risk-of-bias appraisal was not the primary objective of this scoping review. Instead, the synthesis emphasizes reporting completeness, design heterogeneity, missing feasibility fields, and uncertainty around quantitative estimates. This approach is appropriate for mapping a heterogeneous field, but it limits causal inference and prevents strong claims about efficacy.

\section{Results}
\subsection{Study selection and corpus}
\input{tables/table1_baseline}

The included evidence base comprised 65 reports published from 2000 to 2026, with an aggregate extractable sample of 2,188 participants. The corpus was not a coherent set of comparable trials. It combined observational physiology reports such as Durand et al.'s work on exercise-induced arterial hypoxemia \parencite{durand_f_and_mucci_p_and_prefaut_c_evidence_2000}, crossover experiments such as Sakamoto et al.'s hyperventilation studies \parencite{sakamoto_a_and_naito_h_and_chow_cm_hyperventilation_2014,sakamoto_a_and_naito_h_and_chow_cm_hyperventilation-induced_2015,sakamoto_a_and_naito_h_and_chow_cm_effects_2018,sakamoto_hyperventilation-aided_2020}, and training trials such as Woorons et al.'s voluntary-hypoventilation programs \parencite{woorons_effects_2008,woorons_hypoventilation_2016,woorons_physiological_2019,woorons_transferable_2020,woorons_x_and_faucher_c_and_dufour_sp_and_brocherie_f_and_robach_p_and_connes_p_and_brugniaux_jv_and_verges_s_and_gaston_af_and_millet_g_and_dupuy_o_and_pichon_a_hypoventilation_2024}. \subsection{How the evidence base is structured}
\input{tables/table2_map}

Table~\ref{tab:evidence-map} summarizes the map. Crossover designs formed the largest detailed design group, and this matters because acute within-participant experiments answer different questions from repeated training trials. Kacoglu et al. tested brief hyperventilation before jump and sprint tasks \parencite{kacoglu_acute_2017}; Gray et al. tested short pre-race breathing routines in swimming \parencite{gray_pre-race_2018}; Kasap and Aydin compared box breathing and six-breaths-per-minute breathing during post-HIIT recovery \parencite{kasap_m_and_aydin_gr_box_2025}. These papers are useful for acute strategy questions, but they do not provide the same evidence as repeated hypoventilation training trials in swimmers, rugby players, basketball players, ice-hockey players, soccer players, and judo athletes \parencite{woorons_hypoventilation_2016,trincat_repeated-sprint_2017,fornasier-santos_repeated-sprint_2018,lapointe_impact_2020,brocherie_effects_2023,ait_ali_braham_m_and_ouchen_y_and_woorons_x_effects_2024,lorinczi_additional_2025}.

The sport distribution also shaped interpretation. Cycling studies included acute hyperventilation or breath-holding physiology \parencite{fujii_effect_2015,dobashi_voluntary_2021,grossman_kj_and_lim_dj_and_murias_jm_and_belfry_gr_effect_2021} and hypoventilation training transfer questions \parencite{woorons_physiological_2019,woorons_transferable_2020}. Swimming studies included apnea, controlled-frequency breathing, pranayama, and hypoventilation trials \parencite{guimard_acute_2014,stavrou_v_and_toubekis_ag_and_karetsi_e_changes_2015,burtch_controlled_2017,hakked_cs_and_balakrishnan_r_and_krishnamurthy_mn_yogic_2017,trincat_repeated-sprint_2017,robertson_effects_2020}. Strength or resistance contexts were narrower: Sakamoto et al. tested hyperventilation-aided recovery during bench press and leg press \parencite{sakamoto_hyperventilation-aided_2020}, Deniz and Erdemir compared breathing strategies during bench press \parencite{deniz_ie_and_erdemir_i_influence_2025}, and Katz et al. tested a diaphragmatic breathing warm-up before deadlift \parencite{katz_ns_and_rossow_lm_and_fahs_ca_acute_2026}.

\subsection{Voluntary hyperventilation}
Hyperventilation was used mainly as an acute strategy. Sakamoto et al. reported that pre-exercise hyperventilation improved repeated cycling sprint performance in one protocol \parencite{sakamoto_a_and_naito_h_and_chow_cm_hyperventilation_2014}, but the same group found that hyperventilation-induced alkalosis did not prevent fatigue in maximal voluntary contraction testing \parencite{sakamoto_a_and_naito_h_and_chow_cm_hyperventilation-induced_2015}. Their later cycling experiment suggested that hyperventilation duration matters: 15 seconds and 45 seconds during recovery did not reproduce the expected ergogenic effect, whereas the authors interpreted approximately 30 seconds as a more plausible target for that exercise type \parencite{sakamoto_a_and_naito_h_and_chow_cm_effects_2018}. In resistance exercise, Sakamoto et al. reported more repetitions and better maintenance of lifting velocity after hyperventilation-aided recovery than after spontaneous breathing \parencite{sakamoto_hyperventilation-aided_2020}.

Other hyperventilation studies were less supportive or targeted different outcomes. Fujii et al. and Dobashi et al. showed that hypocapnic hyperventilation shifted metabolic responses during Wingate exercise without clearly improving exercise performance \parencite{fujii_effect_2015,dobashi_voluntary_2021}. Jacob et al. reported improved 50-m front-crawl performance after pre-exercise hyperventilation \parencite{jacob_pre-exercise_2015}, whereas Fesseler et al. did not support pre-exercise hyperventilation for inclined sprint-interval performance \parencite{fesseler_l_and_patzak_a_and_heinz_v_and_le_ns_and_opatz_o_and_pilz_n_and_bothe_tl_effect_2026}. Malakhov et al. found worse balance metrics after hyperventilation relative to quiet breathing \parencite{malakhov_m_and_makarenkova_e_and_melnikov_a_influence_2014}, which is a reminder that an arousal or acid-base manipulation may be undesirable when postural control is central.

\subsection{Hypoventilation and end-expiratory breath holding}
The clearest sport-training cluster concerned voluntary hypoventilation, often at low lung volume. Woorons et al. tested four weeks of low-pulmonary-volume voluntary hypoventilation in runners \parencite{woorons_effects_2008}, five weeks of supramaximal hypoventilation training in swimmers \parencite{woorons_hypoventilation_2016}, and repeated-sprint cycling or running protocols in later work \parencite{woorons_acute_2017,woorons_cerebral_2019,woorons_physiological_2019,woorons_transferable_2020}. Trincat et al. found that hypoventilation-induced hypoxia during swimming repeated-sprint training increased the number of sprints completed in a post-training test \parencite{trincat_repeated-sprint_2017}. Fornasier-Santos et al. reported improved running repeated-sprint ability in rugby players after repeated-sprint training with voluntary hypoventilation \parencite{fornasier-santos_repeated-sprint_2018}. Lapointe et al. reported basketball-specific repeated-sprint changes alongside muscle oxygenation, myoelectrical, and potassium-related measures \parencite{lapointe_impact_2020}.

More recent reports extended this line into team and combat sports. Brocherie et al. reported improved off-ice repeated-sprint ability in youth ice-hockey players after off-season RSH-VHL \parencite{brocherie_effects_2023}. Ait Ali Braham et al. compared low- and high-lung-volume hypoventilation strategies in female soccer players and reported gains in repeated-sprint ability, with stronger hypoxemia under the low-lung-volume condition \parencite{ait_ali_braham_m_and_ouchen_y_and_woorons_x_effects_2024}. Woorons et al. reported that elite judo athletes improved the second half of a repeated-sprint rowing test after four weeks of RSH-VHL with maximal end-expiratory breath holding \parencite{woorons_x_and_faucher_c_and_dufour_sp_and_brocherie_f_and_robach_p_and_connes_p_and_brugniaux_jv_and_verges_s_and_gaston_af_and_millet_g_and_dupuy_o_and_pichon_a_hypoventilation_2024}. Lorinczi et al. reported additional repeated-sprint benefits in semiprofessional soccer players when prolonged end-expiratory breath holding was added to repeated-sprint training \parencite{lorinczi_additional_2025}. This cluster is therefore promising, but it is also the cluster where oxygen saturation, breath-hold timing, supervision, and stopping rules are most essential to report.

\subsection{Breath holding, apnea, and freediving-related routines}
Breath-holding and apnea studies were not interchangeable with hypoventilation training. Guimard et al. examined acute apnea swimming responses \parencite{guimard_acute_2014}; Lim et al. and Grossman et al. used periodic breath holding during heavy-intensity cycling or Fartlek-style exercise \parencite{lim_physiological_2018,grossman_kj_and_lim_dj_and_murias_jm_and_belfry_gr_effect_2021}; Bouten et al. reported that acute apnea did not improve 3-km cycling time-trial performance \parencite{bouten_acute_2020}; Robertson et al. tested pre-race apneas before 400-m freestyle swimming \parencite{robertson_effects_2020}. These papers show why timing matters: apnea can be used as a pre-performance stimulus, an exercise constraint, or a recovery-related maneuver, but those uses impose different physiological demands.

Freediving and hook-breathing reports contributed a separate safety-relevant thread. Lindholm and Nyrén examined glossopharyngeal breathing in breath-hold divers \parencite{lindholm_p_and_nyren_s_studies_2005}. Fernandez et al. reported that hook breathing facilitated oxygen-saturation recovery after deep dives in freedivers with slow recovery \parencite{fernandez_hook_2019}. DeAsis-Fernandez et al. reported faster oxygen-saturation recovery after a -40 m apnea dive with the hook maneuver, while ultrasound B-lines and pulmonary-edema-related events were recorded in the safety extraction \parencite{deasis_fernandez_effects_2025}. The apnea-specific papers therefore should not be used as generic evidence for breathing interventions in land-based sport; they are best read as sport-specific evidence where hypoxemia, pressure, and recovery protocols are central.

\subsection{Slow-paced, box, coherent, and diaphragmatic breathing}
Slow-paced and related breathing routines were usually framed around autonomic regulation, recovery, stress response, or precision performance. Laborde et al. found that slow-paced breathing after physical exertion improved inhibition outcomes and produced a small favorable effect for burpee performance in the extractable data \parencite{laborde_influence_2019}. You et al. showed that a brief slow-paced breathing session increased vagally mediated heart-rate-variability indices in athletes \parencite{you_m_and_laborde_s_and_ackermann_s_and_borges_u_and_dosseville_f_and_mosley_e_influence_2024}. Iskra et al. compared slow-paced breathing at 6 cycles per minute with faster paced breathing at 15 cycles per minute and reported different autonomic patterns \parencite{iskra_m_and_laborde_s_and_poppa_t_and_salvotti_c_and_weinand_e_and_raab_m_and_voigt_l_specifying_2026}.

The applied sport findings were mixed because the target tasks differed. Pelka et al. studied slow-paced breathing as a recovery-oriented psychological relaxation technique \parencite{pelka_acute_2017}. Conlon et al. found that a five-minute slow diaphragmatic breathing intervention did not clearly improve shooting accuracy relative to a 12-cycles-per-minute paced-breathing comparator, although RMSSD was extractable as a physiological outcome \parencite{conlon_pulling_2022}. Buxton et al. tested 4-7-8 slow breathing during inter-set recovery in back squat performance \parencite{buxton_effects_2024}. Kasap and Aydin compared box breathing with six-breaths-per-minute breathing after HIIT and found physiological recovery differences that depended on the breathing pattern \parencite{kasap_m_and_aydin_gr_box_2025}. Chiron et al. tested coherent breathing in elite runners during an eight-day intervention \parencite{chiron_f_and_guloren_b_and_erblang_m_and_poirier_c_and_elbaz_m_and_servonnet_a_and_gaudin_m_and_le_coz_c_and_lopes_p_and_chennaoui_m_and_hanon_c_and_leger_d_and_thomas_c_exploring_2026}. Jones et al. reported that box breathing and cyclic sighing accelerated parasympathetic reactivation after maximal exertion in tactical athletes \parencite{jones_effectiveness_2026}. Raidl et al., by contrast, reported no evidence that slow-paced breathing improved recovery after sprint-interval training \parencite{raidl_p_and_wessner_b_and_laister_s_and_csapo_r_no_2026}.

Diaphragmatic breathing was narrower but practically relevant. Sikora et al. observed that diaphragmatic breathing patterns in endurance-trained athletes were associated with better pulmonary-function measures than thoracic patterns \parencite{sikora_m_and_mikolajczyk_r_and_lakomy_o_and_karpinski_j_and_zebrowska_a_and_kostorz-nosal_s_and_jastrzebski_d_influence_2024}. Katz et al. reported a small acute increase in deadlift 1RM after a five-minute diaphragmatic breathing warm-up \parencite{katz_ns_and_rossow_lm_and_fahs_ca_acute_2026}. These findings justify targeted trials, but observational breathing-pattern associations and acute lifting effects should not be merged into one claim.

\subsection{Pranayama, yogic, Wim Hof-type, and composite routines}
Composite routines were harder to interpret because breathing was often combined with traditions, meditation, cold exposure, posture, or breath holds. Stavrou et al. and Hakked et al. reported improved pulmonary-function outcomes in young competitive swimmers after yogic or pranayama-style breathing programs \parencite{stavrou_v_and_toubekis_ag_and_karetsi_e_changes_2015,hakked_cs_and_balakrishnan_r_and_krishnamurthy_mn_yogic_2017}. Vaidya et al. tested yoga in cricket bowlers \parencite{vaidya_ss_and_agarwal_b_and_singh_y_and_mullerpatan_r_effect_2021}, and Bahenský et al. reported altered ventilatory patterns after a yoga-based breathing intervention in young endurance athletes \parencite{bahensky_p_and_bunc_v_and_malatova_r_and_marko_d_and_grosicki_gj_and_schuster_j_impact_2021}. Devipriya et al. reported immediate reaction-time improvements after Bhramari pranayama in young adults \parencite{t_d_and_r_d_and_r_a_s_and_r_p_and_k_n_gk_evaluation_2026}.

Wim Hof-type interventions illustrate the same interpretive problem. Citherlet et al. tested an acute Wim Hof breathing method before repeated sprinting \parencite{citherlet_t_and_crettaz_von_roten_f_and_kayser_b_and_guex_k_acute_2021}; Marko et al. found that four weeks of Wim Hof breathing did not clearly improve breathing economy during exercise in adolescent runners \parencite{marko_d_and_bahensky_p_and_bunc_v_and_grosicki_gj_and_vondrasek_jd_does_2022}; Fox et al. tested a semi-randomized Wim Hof method intervention that included more than isolated breathing practice \parencite{fox_n_and_biddell_h_and_king_j_semi-randomised_2025}. These reports are valuable, but their mechanisms cannot be assigned to breathing alone unless protocols isolate the active components.

\subsection{Exploratory efficacy synthesis}
\input{tables/table3_meta}

Table~\ref{tab:meta} reports the exploratory quantitative syntheses. The pooled performance estimate was close to zero and uncertain. This average is unsurprising once the source studies are read by technique: Malakhov et al.'s balance outcomes after hyperventilation favored quiet breathing \parencite{malakhov_m_and_makarenkova_e_and_melnikov_a_influence_2014}, Burtch et al.'s controlled-frequency breathing trial reduced inspiratory muscle fatigue without improving swimming performance \parencite{burtch_controlled_2017}, Woorons et al.'s RSH-VHL training studies produced favorable repeated-sprint signals \parencite{woorons_physiological_2019,woorons_x_and_faucher_c_and_dufour_sp_and_brocherie_f_and_robach_p_and_connes_p_and_brugniaux_jv_and_verges_s_and_gaston_af_and_millet_g_and_dupuy_o_and_pichon_a_hypoventilation_2024}, and Katz et al. found a small deadlift effect after diaphragmatic breathing \parencite{katz_ns_and_rossow_lm_and_fahs_ca_acute_2026}. A single pooled performance estimate therefore conceals more than it explains.

The physiological synthesis was directionally positive but highly heterogeneous. The most favorable extractable physiological effects included pulmonary-function outcomes in Stavrou et al. and Hakked et al. \parencite{stavrou_v_and_toubekis_ag_and_karetsi_e_changes_2015,hakked_cs_and_balakrishnan_r_and_krishnamurthy_mn_yogic_2017}, HRV outcomes in You et al. and Iskra et al. \parencite{you_m_and_laborde_s_and_ackermann_s_and_borges_u_and_dosseville_f_and_mosley_e_influence_2024,iskra_m_and_laborde_s_and_poppa_t_and_salvotti_c_and_weinand_e_and_raab_m_and_voigt_l_specifying_2026}, and pulmonary-function differences by breathing pattern in Sikora et al. \parencite{sikora_m_and_mikolajczyk_r_and_lakomy_o_and_karpinski_j_and_zebrowska_a_and_kostorz-nosal_s_and_jastrzebski_d_influence_2024}. Physiological outcomes were mechanistically closer to breathing manipulation than performance outcomes, but they spanned oxygenation, ventilation, pulmonary function, HRV, heart rate, blood pressure, and perceptual variables; they should not be treated as one construct.

\subsection{Feasibility and safety}
\input{tables/table4_safety}

Table~\ref{tab:safety} shows that feasibility and safety reporting lagged behind efficacy reporting. Hakked et al. reported 100\% adherence for pranayama/yogic breathing \parencite{hakked_cs_and_balakrishnan_r_and_krishnamurthy_mn_yogic_2017}, but extractable adherence was rare. Dropout or exclusion information was more common but often reflected technical issues, unrelated injuries, or study logistics rather than intervention tolerability: Burtch et al. cited travel, illness, and non-compliance with training sessions \parencite{burtch_controlled_2017}; Sakamoto et al. excluded participants because of transducer error \parencite{sakamoto_a_and_naito_h_and_chow_cm_effects_2018}; Iskra et al. and Jones et al. reported technical exclusions affecting physiological datasets \parencite{iskra_m_and_laborde_s_and_poppa_t_and_salvotti_c_and_weinand_e_and_raab_m_and_voigt_l_specifying_2026,jones_effectiveness_2026}.

Adverse-event reporting was too sparse to support strong reassurance. Sakamoto et al. explicitly reported no hyperventilation-related discomfort in the resistance-exercise recovery study \parencite{sakamoto_hyperventilation-aided_2020}, and Tomita et al. reported completion without adverse events in the focused-attention/controlled-breathing protocol \parencite{tomita_y_and_yokoo_m_and_shimoda_k_and_iizuka_t_and_sakamoto_e_and_irisawa_k_and_tozato_f_and_tsuchiya_k_focused_2026}. By contrast, DeAsis-Fernandez et al. reported pulmonary-edema-related findings in a freediving hook-maneuver context \parencite{deasis_fernandez_effects_2025}. This contrast should guide future reporting: land-based slow-breathing recovery studies, hypoventilation repeated-sprint studies, and deep-apnea studies need different adverse-event definitions and monitoring thresholds.

\section{Discussion}
\subsection{Principal findings}
This review maps a field with enough activity to justify technique-specific research, but not enough consistency to justify a single practice recommendation. The strongest narrative cluster is not generic breathwork; it is voluntary hypoventilation or end-expiratory breath holding embedded in repeated-sprint training, with favorable signals reported by Trincat et al., Fornasier-Santos et al., Lapointe et al., Brocherie et al., Ait Ali Braham et al., Woorons et al., and Lorinczi et al. \parencite{trincat_repeated-sprint_2017,fornasier-santos_repeated-sprint_2018,lapointe_impact_2020,brocherie_effects_2023,ait_ali_braham_m_and_ouchen_y_and_woorons_x_effects_2024,woorons_x_and_faucher_c_and_dufour_sp_and_brocherie_f_and_robach_p_and_connes_p_and_brugniaux_jv_and_verges_s_and_gaston_af_and_millet_g_and_dupuy_o_and_pichon_a_hypoventilation_2024,lorinczi_additional_2025}. The slow-paced and diaphragmatic literature is better interpreted as regulation and recovery evidence, with HRV and stress-response outcomes in You et al., Iskra et al., Chiron et al., Jones et al., and Kasap and Aydin \parencite{you_m_and_laborde_s_and_ackermann_s_and_borges_u_and_dosseville_f_and_mosley_e_influence_2024,iskra_m_and_laborde_s_and_poppa_t_and_salvotti_c_and_weinand_e_and_raab_m_and_voigt_l_specifying_2026,chiron_f_and_guloren_b_and_erblang_m_and_poirier_c_and_elbaz_m_and_servonnet_a_and_gaudin_m_and_le_coz_c_and_lopes_p_and_chennaoui_m_and_hanon_c_and_leger_d_and_thomas_c_exploring_2026,jones_effectiveness_2026,kasap_m_and_aydin_gr_box_2025}. Hyperventilation evidence is more conditional: Sakamoto et al. reported benefits in selected repeated-sprint or resistance-recovery contexts \parencite{sakamoto_a_and_naito_h_and_chow_cm_hyperventilation_2014,sakamoto_hyperventilation-aided_2020}, but Fujii et al., Dobashi et al., Sakamoto et al., and Fesseler et al. show that metabolic shifts do not automatically translate into performance improvement \parencite{fujii_effect_2015,dobashi_voluntary_2021,sakamoto_a_and_naito_h_and_chow_cm_effects_2018,fesseler_l_and_patzak_a_and_heinz_v_and_le_ns_and_opatz_o_and_pilz_n_and_bothe_tl_effect_2026}.

\subsection{Comparison with previous literature}
The findings align with Laborde et al.'s conclusion that breathing techniques in sport should be separated by technique, dose, and context rather than pooled into a broad claim \parencite{Laborde31122024}. The present review extends that conclusion by showing where the map is dense enough for targeted trials and where it remains descriptive. Laborde et al. focused on physical sport performance; this review also charts feasibility, dropout, adverse events, intervention timing, sport context, and physiological outcomes. That broader lens explains why a near-null pooled performance estimate can coexist with plausible technique-specific signals in repeated-sprint hypoventilation, autonomic recovery, pulmonary-function outcomes, and selected strength tasks.

The slow-breathing findings are also consistent with mechanistic literature outside sport. Zaccaro et al. reviewed slow breathing below 10 breaths per minute and described autonomic, central nervous system, and psychological changes, including increased HRV and respiratory sinus arrhythmia, EEG changes, and reduced arousal-related symptoms in healthy participants \parencite{zaccaro_how_2018}. That framework supports interpreting You et al., Iskra et al., Jones et al., Chiron et al., and Kasap and Aydin as regulation and recovery studies rather than direct performance-enhancement trials \parencite{you_m_and_laborde_s_and_ackermann_s_and_borges_u_and_dosseville_f_and_mosley_e_influence_2024,iskra_m_and_laborde_s_and_poppa_t_and_salvotti_c_and_weinand_e_and_raab_m_and_voigt_l_specifying_2026,jones_effectiveness_2026,chiron_f_and_guloren_b_and_erblang_m_and_poirier_c_and_elbaz_m_and_servonnet_a_and_gaudin_m_and_le_coz_c_and_lopes_p_and_chennaoui_m_and_hanon_c_and_leger_d_and_thomas_c_exploring_2026,kasap_m_and_aydin_gr_box_2025}. Fincham et al.'s meta-analysis of randomized breathwork trials reported lower self-reported stress, anxiety, and depression after breathwork than control conditions, while also emphasizing heterogeneity, risk-of-bias concerns, and limited adverse-event reporting \parencite{fincham_effect_2023}. The parallel with the present review is direct: breathing practices may be useful, but translation requires precise intervention descriptions, credible controls, and safety reporting.

\subsection{Implications for practice}
The practical message is specificity. For repeated-sprint training in sports such as swimming, rugby, basketball, ice hockey, soccer, and judo, the most relevant evidence comes from hypoventilation and end-expiratory breath-holding protocols \parencite{trincat_repeated-sprint_2017,fornasier-santos_repeated-sprint_2018,lapointe_impact_2020,brocherie_effects_2023,ait_ali_braham_m_and_ouchen_y_and_woorons_x_effects_2024,woorons_x_and_faucher_c_and_dufour_sp_and_brocherie_f_and_robach_p_and_connes_p_and_brugniaux_jv_and_verges_s_and_gaston_af_and_millet_g_and_dupuy_o_and_pichon_a_hypoventilation_2024,lorinczi_additional_2025}. These protocols should be supervised and should report oxygen saturation, symptoms, and stopping criteria. For post-exertion autonomic recovery, the more relevant evidence comes from slow-paced, box, coherent, cyclic-sighing, or diaphragmatic routines \parencite{you_m_and_laborde_s_and_ackermann_s_and_borges_u_and_dosseville_f_and_mosley_e_influence_2024,kasap_m_and_aydin_gr_box_2025,jones_effectiveness_2026,chiron_f_and_guloren_b_and_erblang_m_and_poirier_c_and_elbaz_m_and_servonnet_a_and_gaudin_m_and_le_coz_c_and_lopes_p_and_chennaoui_m_and_hanon_c_and_leger_d_and_thomas_c_exploring_2026}. For acute ergogenic use of hyperventilation, practitioners should be cautious because benefits appear task- and timing-dependent, and balance or precision demands may make hyperventilation counterproductive \parencite{sakamoto_a_and_naito_h_and_chow_cm_hyperventilation_2014,sakamoto_hyperventilation-aided_2020,malakhov_m_and_makarenkova_e_and_melnikov_a_influence_2014,fesseler_l_and_patzak_a_and_heinz_v_and_le_ns_and_opatz_o_and_pilz_n_and_bothe_tl_effect_2026}.

\subsection{Implications for research}
Future trials should stop asking whether breathwork works and instead test named techniques against mechanism-matched outcomes. A hypoventilation training trial should specify lung volume, breath-hold timing, sprint structure, oxygen saturation, supervision, and stopping criteria. A slow-breathing recovery trial should specify respiratory frequency, inhalation-exhalation ratio, session timing, use of cues or biofeedback, and whether the primary target is HRV, perceived recovery, stress, or subsequent performance. A hyperventilation trial should specify duration, timing relative to exertion, target ventilation, symptoms, and whether the expected mechanism is acid-base change, arousal, or recovery between efforts. Composite interventions such as yoga-based breathing and Wim Hof-type methods should either isolate the breathing component or explicitly state that the intervention is multicomponent \parencite{bahensky_p_and_bunc_v_and_malatova_r_and_marko_d_and_grosicki_gj_and_schuster_j_impact_2021,citherlet_t_and_crettaz_von_roten_f_and_kayser_b_and_guex_k_acute_2021,marko_d_and_bahensky_p_and_bunc_v_and_grosicki_gj_and_vondrasek_jd_does_2022,fox_n_and_biddell_h_and_king_j_semi-randomised_2025}.

Feasibility and safety should be treated as outcomes, not annotations. The next generation of studies should report adherence, acceptability, dropout reasons, intervention fidelity, adverse-event definitions, symptom checklists, and event denominators. This is especially important for interventions that deliberately alter carbon dioxide, oxygen saturation, intrathoracic pressure, or consciousness-related sensations.

\subsection{Limitations}
This review did not conduct formal risk-of-bias appraisal. That is consistent with its scoping purpose, but it limits causal interpretation. The eligibility criteria were broad enough to include acute crossover experiments, training trials, observational physiology studies, and composite interventions, which increased heterogeneity. Several extraction fields were incompletely reported in the source papers, especially adherence, intervention fidelity, symptoms, and adverse-event monitoring. The exploratory Hedges' $g$ and risk-ratio syntheses depend on extractable group-level data and should be read as adjuncts to the map, not as definitive estimates of clinical or sport-practice effectiveness.

\section{Conclusions}
Breathing techniques in sport are not one intervention. The mapped evidence supports technique-specific research: repeated-sprint hypoventilation and end-expiratory breath holding deserve targeted sport-training trials; slow-paced, box, coherent, and diaphragmatic breathing deserve mechanism-matched recovery and regulation trials; hyperventilation requires careful task selection and safety monitoring; composite routines require clearer component reporting. Current pooled estimates are uncertain, and feasibility and safety reporting are too sparse for strong implementation claims. Future research should prioritize precise protocols, credible comparators, complete adherence and adverse-event reporting, and outcomes matched to the proposed mechanism.

\section{Inclusion Criteria}
Eligible studies included healthy participants, athletes, exercisers, trained participants, recreationally active participants, sport students, or healthy volunteers. Mixed samples were eligible when data for healthy, athletic, trained, or physically active participants were reported separately, or when the sample was not primarily clinical.

Eligible concepts were breathing interventions, breathing maneuvers, or breathing-related exposures relevant to sport, exercise, or performance. Eligible strategies included breath holding, apnea or apnoea, voluntary hypoventilation, reduced-frequency breathing during exercise, voluntary hyperventilation, hypocapnic hyperventilation, deep or rapid breathing intended to alter ventilation, paced breathing, slow-paced breathing, coherent breathing, resonance breathing, metronome breathing, controlled breathing, diaphragmatic or abdominal breathing, pranayama or yogic breathing, Bhramari breathing, alternate-nostril or uni-nostril breathing, Wim Hof-type breathing, box breathing, hook breathing, and other structured breath-regulation practices. HRV biofeedback or respiratory biofeedback was eligible when the biofeedback was used to guide, monitor, or train breathing rather than acting as the main physiological intervention.

Eligible contexts included sport, exercise, training, recovery, competition, and performance-relevant laboratory contexts. Eligible outcomes included sport-, exercise-, or physical-performance-relevant outcomes such as endurance, sprint performance, time-trial performance, repeated-sprint ability, power, strength, jump performance, swimming, cycling, running performance, skill accuracy, shooting performance, reaction time, balance, fatigue resistance, recovery performance, or other objective or validated performance-related outcomes. Physiological, psychological, perceptual, feasibility, adherence, or safety outcomes were eligible as secondary outcomes when the study also had a sport-, exercise-, or performance-relevant context.

Original empirical studies of any design were eligible, including randomized controlled trials, crossover studies, non-randomized experimental studies, before-after studies, observational studies, and pilot studies. Studies were not excluded solely because they were small, acute, non-randomized, or exploratory, provided they met the population, concept, context, and outcome criteria.

\section{Exclusion Criteria}
Studies were excluded if they were reviews, systematic reviews, scoping reviews, meta-analyses, narrative reviews, editorials, commentaries, letters without original data, protocols without results, or theoretical papers.

Studies were excluded if they were conducted primarily in clinical or patient populations unless a healthy, athletic, trained, recreationally active, or physically active subgroup was reported separately. Examples of excluded clinical populations included COPD, asthma, sleep apnea, stroke, pulmonary disease, dyspnea-related conditions, cardiac disease, neurological disease, and rehabilitation-only samples.

Studies were excluded if they did not test or observe a breathing maneuver, breathing intervention, or breath-regulation strategy. Respiratory muscle training, inspiratory muscle training, expiratory muscle training, respiratory loading, resisted breathing, pressure-threshold loading, aided inspiration, oxygen supplementation, hypoxic chambers, altitude tents, altered gas mixtures, or other device-based interventions were excluded when the device or altered gas condition was the main intervention rather than self-regulated breathing. Meditation, relaxation, mindfulness, yoga, or biofeedback interventions were excluded when the breathing component was absent, not described, or could not be separated from a broader multicomponent intervention.

Studies were excluded if they had no sport, exercise, physical-performance, recovery, competition, or performance-relevant context, or if they reported only physiological, psychological, autonomic, biochemical, or perceptual outcomes without a performance-relevant task or outcome. Abstracts without sufficient data, unavailable full texts, duplicate records, animal studies, non-human studies, and non-English articles were also excluded when applicable.
